Pronouns and Demonstrative Adjectives

Read the following sentences:

The goddess herself gave him the gift.

The philosophers teach them.

Be sure that you understand each of the underlined words in English.

"Herself" is an Intensive pronoun in this context. "Him" and "them" are third person Personal pronouns.

"Herself" in the following sentence is Reflexive:

She saw herself in the mirror.

Be sure that you understand the difference between Intensive and Reflexive pronouns in English. [Why in English can they look the same? In Greek why should they be different forms?]

Now read the following sentences from Thrasymachus, noting the underlined words:

αὐτὴ μὲν ἐγὼ οὐχ ὁρῶ αὐτὸν, ἴσως δ’ ἡ Ἀφροδίτη ὁρᾷ.

καὶ ταῦτα ἐστὶ τὸ πρόβατα αὐτοῦ.

This pronoun (αὐτός, αὐτή, αὐτό) can be used in three ways:

  1. As the Intensive pronoun and adjective when not between an article and its noun (in the so called "predicate position"). αὐτὴ ἐγώ = I myself

  2. As an adjective meaning "the same" when placed between the article and its noun (in the so-called "attributive position"). ὁρῶ τὸν αὐτὸν θεὸν = I see the same god

  3. As a third-person pronoun when in accusative, genitive, or dative case (one of the "oblique cases"), and not in the attributive position. ὁρῶ αὐτόν = I see him

It is very important to recognize the forms of αὐτός, αὐτή, αὐτό. You must notice the breathing and accent marks to distinguish it from the forms of οὗτος, αὕτη, τοῦτο, which means "this". [What are some English derivatives of αὐτός, αὐτή, αὐτό?]

An example of all three uses of this word in one sentence is:

τῇ αὐτῇ ἡμέρᾳ, Κῦρος αὐτὸς ὁρᾷ αὐτους.
On the same day, Cyrus himself sees them.

See Thrasymachus pp. 20 and 200 for the forms of this word. (Note the nominative and accusative neuter singular ending –ο. This pattern will become familiar to you).

On the same pages, you will find the two Demonstrative pronouns/adjectives, οὗτος, αὕτη, τοῦτο ("this") and ἐκεῖνος, –η, –ο ("that").

[What declensions are these demonstrative adjectives? Are there any slight differences between these and other similar adjectives?]

To help identify case, number and gender correctly, notice when τουτ- is the stem and when ταυτ- is the stem. Formulate a rule.

Accent and breathing marks are crucial to the correct identifications of these pronouns/adjectives.

[Have you seen before the pattern of an initial "τ" in all forms but nominative masculine and feminine?]

When a form of οὗτος or ἐκεῖνος is used as a demonstrative adjective ("this" and "that"), it is not placed between the article and its noun.

τίς ἐστιν οὗτος ὁ ἀνήρ;
Who is this man?

ἐκεῖναι αἱ γυναίκες εἰσιν ἀγαθαί.
Those women are good.

Note the difference among the following:

τί ἐστιν ἀνήρ;
What is a man?

τίς ἐστιν ὁ ἀνήρ;
Who is the man?

τίς ἐστιν οὗτος ὁ ἀνήρ;
Who is this man?

τίς ἐστιν ἐκεῖνος ὁ ἀνήρ;
Who is that man?

Pronoun/Adjective Review

Before we continue, be absolutely sure that you understand, in English and in Greek, the pronouns we have discussed up to this point. These words are so common that recognizing them correctly is crucial to your understanding of Greek. As you review them, you will be reinforcing your declension endings.

See Thrasymachus pp. 200-203 for paradigms.

Interrogative and Indefinite

τίς, τί (who, what); τις, τι (a certain). Third declension. The only difference between them is the accent. The base is τιν– (note when the ν drops out).

Personal Pronouns

First and second person, singular and plural: ἐγώ (I), ἡμεῖς (we), σύ (you), ὑμεῖς (you, plural). Mix of first/second and third declension, some variations.

Third person: αὐτός, αὐτή, αὐτό (when in an oblique case and in the predicate position). First/second declension.

Intensive Pronoun

αὐτός, αὐτή, αὐτό (when in the the predicate position). First/second declension.

Review the section above about how this pronoun can be used in three ways.

Reflexive Pronoun

ἐμαυτόν, –ήν = myself. σεαυτόν, –ήν = yourself. ἑαυτόν, –ήν = himself, herself. First/second declension.

Demonstratives

οὗτος, αὕτη, τοῦτο = this. ἐκεῖνος, –η, –ον = that. ὅδε, ἥδε, τόδε = this, that, the following. First/second declension. [Look carefully at the forms of ὅδε. How do you form this word?]

You will need to have the pronouns and demonstratives firmly fixed in your mind, both the "concept" and the forms, before we move on to relative pronouns.
