The Relative Pronoun

Translate the following pairs of sentences into English:

ἡ γυνή ἐστι ἀγαθή. ἡ γυνὴ βλέπει τὸν παῖδα.

ὁρῶ τὴν γυναῖκα . ἡ γυνὴ πέμπει τὰ δῶρα.

[Does each pair read "smoothly?" How might you change each pair so that it becomes one "smooth" English sentence?]

As you have seen, there are several ways of qualifying a noun: with an adjective, for instance, or with another noun in apposition:

ἡ γυνὴ πέμπει τὸ δῶρον.
The woman sends the gift.

ἡ ἀγαθὴ γυνὴ πέμπει τὸ δῶρον.
The good woman sends the gift.

ἡ γυνὴ, μήτηρ μοῦ, πέμπει τὸ δῶρον.
The woman, my mother, sends the gift.

There is another way of qualifying a noun, by the use of a relative clause which in its entirety modifies the noun.

For example:

ἡ γυνὴ ἥ ἐστι ἀγαθὴ πέμπει τὰ δῶρα.
The woman who is good sends the gifts.

[Remember that a clause has a subject and a verb, whereas a phrase does not. Is a relative clause a dependent or independent clause?]

Relative Pronouns (Cont'd)

You can easily see that the relative clause is a dependent clause. It does not stand stand alone as a complete sentence. ("to whom I give the gift" or "whom I see," for examples, are not independent clauses.) Instead, the relative clause qualifies a noun. ("The girl to whom I give the gift" is a particular girl, just like "the tall girl, the short girl" etc.).

English also has relative pronouns in different cases:

  • Nominative: who
  • Accusative: whom
  • Genitive: of whom, whose
  • Dative: to whom

If you look carefully again at the sentences in the exercise above, you will be able to see these rules at work:

  1. A relative pronoun takes its gender and number from its antecedent (the word it refers to).

  2. A relative pronoun takes its case from its function within its own clause.

Here are a few more examples:

οἱ θεοὶ οἵ εἰσιν ἐν οὐρανῷ βλέπουσιν ἡμᾶς.

ὁ θεός ὅς ἐστιν ἐν οὐρανῷ βλέπει ἡμᾶς.

οἱ θεοί παιδεύουσι τοῦς φιλοσόφους οὓς βλέπω.

Review these important points about the relative clause and the relative pronoun:

  • The relative clause is a dependent clause which qualifies a noun.

  • The relative pronoun takes its gender and number from its antecedent but its case from its function within the relative clause.

  • The relative pronoun is second-first declension.

  • Each form of the relative pronoun starts with a vowel and has a rough breathing mark.

Answer Key

    1. ὁ ἀνὴρ ὅς ἐστι ἀγαθὸς παιδεύει τοῦς παῖδας. ὅς is nominative, singular, masculine. The man who is good is teaching the children.

    2. οἱ ἄνδρες οἵ εἰσιν ἀγαθοὶ παιδεύουσι τοῦς παῖδας. οἵ is nominative, plural, masculine. The men who are good teach the children.

    3. οἱ ἄνδρες παιδεύουσι τοὺς παῖδας οἵ εἰσιν ἀγαθοί. οἵ is nominative, plural, masculine. The men teach the children who are good.

    4. οἱ ἄνδρες παιδεύουσι τοὺς παῖδας οὕς αἱ γυναῖκες βλέπουσιν. οὕς is accusative, plural, masculine. The men teach the children whom the women watch.

    5. ὁ ἀνὴρ παιδεύει τὸν παῖδα ὅς ἐστι ἀγαθός. ὅς is nominative, singular, masculine. The man teaches the child who is good.

    6. οἱ ἄνδρες παιδεύουσι τὸν παῖδα ὅν ἡ θεός βλεπει. ὅν is accusative, singular, masculine. The men teach the child whom the god watches.

    7. ὁ ἀνὴρ οὗ τὸ παιδίον παιδεύω, ἐστὶ ἀγαθός. οὗ is genitive, singular, masculine. The man whose child I teach is good.

    8. οἱ ἄνδρες ὧν παιδία παιδεύω εἰσὶν ἀγαθοί. ὧν is genitive, plural, masculine. The men whose children I teach are good.

    9. ἡ γυνὴ ᾗ πέμπω τὸ δῶρόν ἐστι ἀγαθή. ᾗ is dative, singular, feminine. The woman to whom I send the gift is good.

    10. αἱ γυναῖκες αἷς πέμπω τὰ δῶρά εἰσιν ἀγαθαί. αἷς is dative, plural, feminine. The women to whom I give the gifts are good.
